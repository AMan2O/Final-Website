If anybody here would like some unsolicited anecdotal advice about how to start with music design, you've come to the right spot!




"...Why should I listen?"




Should you ask this fair question, Here's some potential considerations:


  • You get melodies or progressions stuck in your head constantly
  • Need a quick and dirty solution for a backing track
  • You want to try making music but are unsure of where to start
  • You're just looking for a 5 minute or so distraction
  • You profoundly don't care either way





"Where do I start?"




You have two main options: Do what I did and just start messing around, or Study up first.


In my experience, I'd categorize the options I saw into three main types:


The Informal The Trackers The Professional
Examples include: Chrome Music Lab, Beepbox Furnace, OpenMPT Reaper, FL Studio



The Informal




This category is great for dipping your toes into the space.


The general vibe of these types are an emphasis on making something to listen to, and they're great for getting ideas out quick.



(If you want some more specific suggestions, click the logo)



This tool, Beepbox.co and its many forks (my personal favorite being jummb.us) are great as you get better and improve.


They start out more involved than things such as Chrome Music Lab's Song Maker, but you can explore the Song Settings and Preference tabs at your own pace as you get more comfortable.




If you're looking for something more specific however, the next choice could be:













The Trackers




This category is great for chiptune and repetition.


They can be more accurately described as sound emulators, and are far more particular as a result.



There's a very real chance that without reading up on a specific tool's documentation (like Furnace's), you'd have no clue what's even on screen (I know I didn't!).


A fun quirk of many trackers is that because of their emulation, many of them are capable of natively running on hardware like an NES or retro computers!


If it is what you're looking for, they're still conceptually simple once you learn the specifics.




The same cannot be said about the last category, however.




The Professional







These are what separates the hobbyist from the audiophile.


These go beyond an abstracted structure, and are instead so free-form that I would recommend studying up music theory first before approaching them.




Powerful DAWs like FL Studio are fully paid, and even the previous example Reaper politely asks after the trial period expires.


With that said however, nothing compares to the mixing and quality control options a good DAW can provide. If you can stick with it, it is unmatched.
